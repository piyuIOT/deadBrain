Hey Aaron,

This is Pratham — I'm not sure if you remember me, but I was one of the three people involved in that Oreo brownie in a cup @ EndureAir Delhi NCR (I was mostly slicing almonds and cashews, but shhhh). Opening with that specific detail to jog your memory a bit.

The primary purpose of this letter is to thank you from the deepest corners of my heart, and to tell you how jealous I am of you — also from the deepest corners of my heart. I'll explain both. Let's get into it.

---

Once upon a time — because that's how all good stories start — there was this girl. An intern with an accent, who worked in my office (and casually taught me what an airfoil was).

This same person then went on a trek to Mt. Kilimanjaro in Tanzania. Boots and crampons! When she came back, she brought a keychain for Saran (ya, I wouldn't mind one next time). Anyway. She also brought back a few photos.

She was showing us these photos — all of it gorgeous and beautiful — until one particular picture started a domino effect that eventually led me to write you this letter. And I have been meaning to write this for a very long time. (More on that later in the letter — aaha, pun!)

The photo was a night sky. Absolutely gorgeous, full of stars. But the main thing — the thing — was that she had captured the Milky Way from her phone. A regular phone. (I've since had the quiet, depressing realization that we could've been seeing skies like this our whole lives if we hadn't so thoroughly polluted everything, but that's a separate conversation.)

At the time, I had the following thoughts, in this exact order:

1. the fuck, it's so fuckin' beautiful
2. no way that's real
3. the fuck it's real
4. no way she got that from a regular camera
5. the fuck it's from her phone
6. there ain't no way she could actually see it — it's just phone tech doing things
7. the fuck — she saw the Milky Way with her naked eye
8. god, in college I used to love watching the sky. I always wanted to see the Milky Way
9. man, she is living my dream
10. god I am so jealous of this girl
11. aww hell, I'm jealous of her accent too
12. huh. I don't like her.

That was the first five minutes. Then, through the following night and a couple of days after:

13. No. I don't like this. I need to go out and take back my Milky Way.
14. I need to travel. Trek mountains. Go places most people don't bother, until I find a sky clear enough to see it for myself.
15. I need to find a trek. I need to do this.

---

I asked this girl how one actually goes about trekking, and she very reasonably said: get a tour package for first-timers, don't go solo, that's how people disappear. Fair. I found one — Valley of Flowers, Uttarakhand. Guide, stay, full deal.

I then spent a couple of weeks trying to find people to come, because I was scared to go alone! A few people said yes, then they said no. Eventually I thought: there's a long weekend coming, too late to convince people now, I'll throw in a few leaves, give myself a full week, and I am going no matter what and no matter who's coming.

I called the tour package guy and told him to scrap the 4-5 tickets I'd been negotiating for — friends, cousins, the whole group — and book me one. Solo. He was confused, but he didn't push it. Good man.

I got back home, announced what I'd done — and immediately, these very same people who couldn't commit for last three weeks all decided yes, now they were in. Every single one of them. One cousin, then another, then one more, then someone's friend. Five people. The trip I had booked alone now had a full group. Off we went.

---

The drive to Govindghat was terrifying. Narrow mountain roads — man I am scored of height (it has nothing to do with how tall I am!). I made it without fainting, which I am counting as a testament to nothing (I had no choice! I paid for it, huh).

We reached, checked in, stayed the night.

Next morning I woke up and got tea. I am not a tea person — but it was fucking cold and I needed something warm to hold, so the tea was basically just a vessel. Then I went to the window, and I am not being dramatic when I say it was the best view I'd had in my life up to that point.

I stood there thinking: *okay. When am I going to see that night sky?* I have a competition to win.

We asked the guide. An hour to the next stop, then a 4-5 hour trek, then we'd reach the base — a place called Gangaria. From there, Valley of Flowers one way, Hemkund Sahib the other.

The walk to Gangaria was also amazing. Roughly 7-8 kilometres, not brutally steep, moderately tiring — and completely stunning. Very outside my comfort zone too, given that I had never been on any trip without my parents up until this point in my life. But I was smiling the entire way. Reached a small settlement in the mountains — shops, trekkers, tour groups everywhere. We had Pahadwala Maggi.

Look, I know it's the same packet, the same two-minute noodles. I know that. And yet.

It was one of the most wonderful days. I was already dying to see what tomorrow looked like.

---

And by evening, I was sitting there looking at the night sky.

Not the Milky Way — not quite what this girl had shown me. But it was pretty fucking good. And there I was, smiling to myself in the dark, thinking about the insane chain of events that brought me here. One photo. That's all it took. One photo from a phone, and I forced myself into this day, a night-long bus ride, then a mountain road that nearly gave me an cardiac attack, then an 8 kilometre trek — arrived somewhere I had never been, hadn't even seen what I came for yet — and I was so full of joy and peace it was genuinely confusing.

you had a fair bit of share into this. And for that, I thank you from the bottom of my heart.
