## Activity 1: COVID-19 Case Study
**Time:** 45 min
**Materials:** Latest gender-wise COVID case data, illustrative version of Minal Dakhave Bhosale's story, newspaper clippings

### Part A — What children know (validation)
Ask children about their lockdown experiences, then what they know about COVID. Write responses on slips/board. Two columns:

| Modes of Infection (संक्रमण के तरीके) | Precautions/Prevention (बचाव के तरीके) |
|--------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| | |

**Goal:** From prevention methods, reverse-engineer the infection mechanism. Key insight: virus enters via nose/mouth into respiratory tract.

Prevention categories:
- **Block contact:** covering body, PPE, not touching infected surfaces
- **Remove/destroy on body:** changing clothes, bathing, washing hands, sanitising
- **Block entry to nose/mouth:** wearing mask, not touching face without washing hands

**Discussion questions:**
- How did the virus affect boys and girls differently?
- Did lockdown affect girls differently from brothers/fathers?
- Share mortality rate data difference between men and women. Why?
- Share data on decline in women's labour force participation. Reasons?

> [!note] Context

## Activity 2: Comparing Curd vs Spoiled Milk
**Time:** 30 min

Start by asking what products are made from milk. Write two columns on board — properties of curd vs spoiled milk.

**Expected outcome:** Both look similar but differ in smell, taste, and edibility (खाद्यता).

**Key explanation:**
- Fungi and bacteria from environment enter milk, multiply, chemically alter components (lactose, fats, proteins)
- When we add a starter (जामन) to make curd, we introduce a **specific beneficial bacterium** in large initial numbers → it multiplies fast → outcompetes harmful microorganisms
- Spoiled milk: random environmental microbes dominate → harmful changes

> [!note] Non-obvious
> The difference between curd and spoiled milk is not just "bacteria present vs absent" — it's *which* bacteria dominates. Controlled inoculation with the right species produces curd; uncontrolled contamination produces spoilage.

## Activity 3: Smell — A Warning?
**Time:** 35 min | **Materials:** Spoiled and fresh food

Referencing Activity 2: ask which property tells us milk has spoiled → **smell**.

How do we detect spoiled food at home? → smell again.

**Discussion:** What are the risks of drinking spoiled milk or eating spoiled food?

**Key point — can you make spoiled food safe by heating it?**

> [!warning] Critical misconception
> **No.** Reheating kills microorganisms but does NOT destroy the toxic substances (विषैले पदार्थ) they have already produced. Eating such food can still cause illness.

**Activity extension:** Children smell fresh vs spoiled food samples and note the difference. Encourage use of smell as an inspection tool.

## Activity 4: Understanding Food Poisoning
**Time:** 20 min

**Discussion questions:**
- What is food poisoning?
- Where do you see it in your homes?
- What causes it?
- Which microorganism causes food poisoning?
- What are its symptoms?

Give children 5 minutes to think, then share.

### Causes of Food Poisoning

**Bacteria (जीवाणु)**
Primary cause. Key pathogens:
- *Salmonella* — most common in USA
- *Campylobacter*
- *Clostridium botulinum* — lethal

**Viruses (वायरस)**
Less common but potentially lethal:
- Norovirus, Sapovirus, Rotavirus, Astrovirus
- Hepatitis A — serious foodborne illness

**Parasites (परजीवी)**
Rare. *Toxoplasma* found in cat litter boxes.

Food can also be contaminated during growing, harvesting, or storage — pathogens transfer surface to surface. Raw ready-to-eat foods are highest risk (pathogens not killed by cooking).

### Symptoms of Food Poisoning
- Weakness (कमजोरी)
- Headache (सिरदर्द)
- Nausea (जी मिचलाना)
- Diarrhoea (दस्त)
- Vomiting (उल्टी)
- Stomach cramps (पेट में ऐंठन)
- Mild fever (हल्का बुखार)
- Loss of appetite (भूख में कमी)

### Prevention
1. Always eat clean and covered food
2. Wash hands before/after eating and before cooking
3. Wash vegetables and fruits thoroughly
4. Cook raw vegetables and meat properly
5. Try to eat fresh food; refrigerate leftovers

## Activity 5: Understanding Food Preservation
**Time:** 20 min

Ask children: what foods are made from milk? (curd, khoya, peda, barfi) → List on board with self-life without refrigeration.

**Core question:** Why do milk-derived products have different shelf lives despite all coming from milk?

**Framework — microorganism multiplication requires:**

```
Infection → Favourable conditions (pH, food, temperature, water) → Multiplication
```

Changing any factor stops multiplication. Common food preservation methods:

| Method | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Boiling milk | Kills bacteria present |
| Making peda | Reduces water content |
| Making pickle (achaar) | Reduces water + changes pH |
| Tetra packaging | Prevents new infection |
| Drying vegetables/meat | Eliminates water entirely |

**Think further:** Why does sugar spoil slower than syrup (chashni) and syrup slower than sharbat?

Reference: https://hi.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/खाद्य_परिरक्षण

## Activity 6: Sugar + Warm Water + Yeast = Bubbles (Proof of Life?)
**Time:** 45 min | **Materials:** Sugar, warm water, yeast, glass

Divide into 3 groups. Each gets a bowl/beaker with warm water:

| Group | Contents |
|-------|----------|
| 1 | Warm water + dry yeast only |
| 2 | Warm water + sugar only |
| 3 | Warm water + sugar + dry yeast |

Observe for bubbles. Groups 1 & 2: no bubbles. Group 3: bubbles (CO₂).

**Conclusions children should draw:**
1. Yeast respires → therefore it is a living organism (no yeast = no bubbles)
2. Yeast needs sugar/food for respiration (no sugar = no bubbles even with yeast)

> [!note] The bubbles are CO₂ — a product of yeast's anaerobic respiration (fermentation). This is the same process used in bread-making and alcohol production.

## Activity 8: Observing Bread Mould (Rhizopus)
**Time:** 35 min | **Materials:** Bread/roti with mould grown 2–3 days prior

**Preparation (2 days before):** Sprinkle water on a bread slice, leave open. Within 1–2 days, white fluffy mould appears, later showing green-blue patches. Optionally keep a mushroom for comparison.

Children observe mould through a **magnifying glass**, focusing on:
- Colour, shape, structure
- Identify two visible parts: long thin stalk + round tip (sporangiophore + sporangium)

Draw the structure on paper, label distinct parts. Compare drawing with Rhizopus diagram in textbook.

If mushroom available: compare microscopic fungus (Rhizopus) with macroscopic fungus (mushroom) — structural similarities and differences.

## Activity 9: Wound Care (Discussion)
**Time:** 45 min | **Materials:** First aid chart/poster

**Discussion questions:**
1. Have you ever had a wound or injury? Share personal experience.
2. What do you do when you get a wound? (Identify common practices — some may be wrong, e.g. applying mud)
3. What is the correct first aid for a wound?
   - Write steps in order from children's prior knowledge
   - Verify against a first aid poster
   - Two categories of steps: **stop bleeding** (e.g. applying pressure) and **prevent infection** (e.g. Dettol wash, bandaging)
4. What is the logic behind each first aid step?
   - Identify which steps relate to microorganisms and infection prevention
   - Discuss **tetanus vaccine** — when and why needed

**First aid box essentials discussed:**
- Anti-infective cleaning agents: Savlon/Dettol, Betadine, Soframycin
- Painkillers: Paracetamol, Ibuprofen
- Antibiotic ointment (Fusidic acid, Mupirocin)
- Crepe bandage, gauze, Band-Aid
- Thermometer

**Snake bite first aid (from poster):**
- Calm the patient
- Immobilise the patient
- Wash wound with water
- Cover with clean cloth
- Remove all jewellery/loosen clothing (swelling may occur)
- Do not move patient
- Do NOT apply tourniquet
- Take to nearest government hospital with anti-venom

## Activity 10: Female-Specific Infections
**Time:** 35 min
**Materials:** Reference material on cervical cancer, UTI, yeast infection

Conduct with support of a specialist/health worker.

### Cervical Cancer (सर्वाइकल कैंसर)
Cancer originating in cells of the cervix (गर्भाशय ग्रीवा) — the lower narrow end of the uterus connecting to the vagina.

**Development:** Cervical cells undergo dysplasia (abnormal changes) → over time, if not removed, become cancerous.

**Two parts of cervix:**
- **Ectocervix** (एक्टोसर्विक्स): outer part, covered in squamous cells
- **Endocervix** (एन्डोसर्विक्स): inner part/canal, covered in columnar glandular cells

**Types:**
- **Squamous cell carcinoma** — ~90% of cervical cancers; from ectocervix cells
- **Adenocarcinoma** — from endocervix glandular cells

**Statistics (USA):** ~14,000 diagnoses/year; most common age 35–44; ~4,000 deaths/year; declining due to screening and HPV vaccine.

**Causative agent:** HPV (Human Papillomavirus)

### UTI — Urinary Tract Infection (मूत्रमार्ग का संक्रमण)
Infection in any part of urinary system — kidneys, bladder, ureters, urethra. Women more susceptible than men.

**Symptoms in women:**
- Burning sensation while urinating
- Frequent/urgent urge to urinate
- Frequent small amounts of urine
- Strong-smelling urine
- Pelvic pain
- Fluctuating body temperature
- Chills and sweating (indicates spread to kidneys)
- Blood in urine (red/orange/pink)

Reference: Urinary Tract Infection (UTI): Causes, Symptoms & Treatment

### Vaginal Yeast Infection (योनि यीस्ट संक्रमण)
**Causative agent:** *Candida* species (fungus)

Reference: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaginal_yeast_infection

## Activity 12: Woman Scientist in Microbiology — Dr. Minal Dakhave Bhosale
**Time:** 20 min

### Story

In March 2020, as COVID-19 deaths were rising globally, India was being criticised for limited testing. Testing kits were imported at **₹4500/kit** with **6-hour result times**.

**Dr. Minal Dakhave Bhosale** — virologist and R&D head at Mylab Discovery Solutions, Pune — saw this as an opportunity to serve her country.

She began work on the testing kit in **February 2020**, when she was **8 months pregnant**. She had extensive prior experience on swine flu at NIV (National Institute of Virology), Pune.

She developed **India's first indigenous COVID-19 testing kit:**

| Parameter | Imported Kit | Minal's Kit |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Cost | ₹4500/kit | ₹1200/100 samples |
| Result time | 6 hours | ~2 hours |
| Development time | — | 6 weeks |

She submitted the kit to India's FDA and CDSCO for commercial approval — and a few hours later, gave birth to her daughter, Jasvi.

> "It was an emergency situation, so I took it as a challenge. I had to serve my country."

**Background:** Studied at Pune University; worked at National Institute of Virology, Pune; worked on swine flu during the 2009 outbreak.

> [!note] Pedagogical intent
> Dr. Minal Dakhave Bhosale demonstrates that women in STEM can achieve extraordinary things, including while navigating personal challenges. Her story directly connects to the COVID case study in Activity 1.
