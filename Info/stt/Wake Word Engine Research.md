# Wake Word Engine Research

> **Context:** Research into how popular wake word engines collect and generate training data, how synthetic audio pipelines work, and what this means for designing the `wake_words` section of `stt_corpus.json`.

---

## Key Finding Upfront

> **You almost certainly don't need to collect real wake word audio from users at all.** Every major open-source wake word engine in 2024–2026 trains entirely (or primarily) on **synthetic audio generated by TTS models**. The entire field has moved to this approach. Human-recorded positives are used for fine-tuning and evaluation, not for bulk training.

---

## 1. How Popular Wake Word Engines Work

### openWakeWord (dscripka) — The Open-Source Standard

**Architecture:** Audio embedding model (pretrained by Google on AudioSet) → small DNN classifier trained per wake word.

**Training data approach: 100% synthetic positives.**
- Uses **Piper TTS** (fast, local neural TTS) to generate thousands of variations of the wake word spoken by different synthetic voices
- Speaker embedding interpolation — blends speaker embeddings to generate "in-between" voices that don't exist in the original TTS training set, massively expanding voice diversity
- Deliberately uses high variance sampling parameters (trades perfect audio quality for more acoustic variation)
- All included models ("Hey Jarvis", "Alexa", "Hey Mycroft") trained with zero real human recordings for positives

**Negative data: 30,000 hours of real audio**
- The model needs to learn not to fire on everything else — this requires huge negative data
- Sources: speech, music, environmental sounds, background noise
- Negative data is real (not synthetic) because the acoustic variety of "everything that isn't the wake word" is too complex to synthesize

**Community-reported training scale:**
- ~320,000 positive synthetic samples
- ~320,000 negative samples
- Train/test split: 300k/20k each
- Final model size: ~200 KB (ONNX format)

**Pipeline summary:**
```
Wake phrase text
  → Piper TTS (50+ voices, varied speaker embeddings)
  → Speed perturbation (0.8x–1.2x)
  → Room impulse response convolution (simulates different rooms)
  → Additive noise (music, speech, environmental)
  → Mel spectrogram features
  → DNN classifier training
  → ONNX export (~200KB)
```

**GitHub:** https://github.com/dscripka/openWakeWord

---

### micro-wake-word (OHF-Voice / Home Assistant)

Designed for ESP32 microcontrollers — extremely resource constrained (TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers).

**Training data: fully synthetic via Piper sample generator**
- Piper generates wake word samples at multiple speeds and pitches
- SpecAugment used to mask time and frequency features (data augmentation without new recordings)
- Negative data: pre-generated spectrogram features hosted on HuggingFace for download — you don't generate negatives yourself

**Real human data:** Home Assistant ran a "Wake Word Collective" campaign in late 2024 to supplement synthetic data with real recordings. Collected **5,800 samples across 30 languages** — used for evaluation and accent generalization, not bulk training.

**Key lesson:** Even with 5,800 real samples they consider this a supplement to synthetic data, not a replacement.

**GitHub:** https://github.com/OHF-Voice/micro-wake-word

---

### Picovoice Porcupine (Commercial)

**Approach: no data collection from the customer at all.**

Porcupine uses transfer learning at inference time — you type the wake phrase as text and it trains a model in seconds. No recordings, no data pipeline, no waiting.

Under the hood, Porcupine maintains a massive pre-trained acoustic model that already knows phoneme acoustics extremely well (trained on thousands of hours of diverse speech). The custom wake word training is purely adapting the final classifier layer using phoneme-level transfer, not training from scratch.

**What this tells you:** If your phoneme acoustic model is good enough (e.g., fine-tuned Whisper encoder), you can reduce the custom wake word problem to a classifier fine-tuning step — not a data collection problem.

**Porcupine benchmarks:** < 0.5 false accepts per hour at < 5% false reject rate on their internal test set.

---

### Snowboy (Retired, but historically important)

Snowboy (Kitt.ai, acquired by Amazon) required:
- **3 recordings** of the wake word from the user, uploaded to their cloud service
- Used template matching (not DNN) — compared incoming audio to stored templates
- Worked surprisingly well for personal use but did not generalize across speakers

**Why it failed at scale:** Template matching means the model is literally the 3 recordings you uploaded. It works for you but nobody else. This is why the field moved to DNN with synthetic data.

---

### Apple "Hey Siri"

Apple published a research paper (2018, updated) with details on their data pipeline:

**Positive data collection:**
- Recorded native speakers in **multiple real-world environments**: kitchen (near and far), car, bedroom, restaurant
- Multiple accents and languages per environment
- Used for training the acoustic model backbone — not just the wake word detector

**Negative data:**
- Thousands of hours of podcasts and non–"Hey Siri" inputs
- Background speech, music, ambient sounds
- Special emphasis on **"confusable phrases"** — phrases that acoustically resemble "Hey Siri" (see below)

**On-device personalization (the clever part):**
- First time you say "Hey Siri" during setup, the phone records 5 samples
- These are stored on-device only — used for speaker-specific fine-tuning via federated learning
- Your personal model is never sent to Apple's servers

**Key lesson:** Even Apple — with enormous resources — relied on a relatively small set of purpose-recorded environment samples for positives, then heavy negative data and on-device personalization to handle speaker variation.

---

### Amazon Alexa

Amazon's ASRU 2020 paper "Eliminating Data Collection Bottleneck for Wake Word Engine Training Using Found and Synthetic Data" describes their approach:

- Used **voice conversion** to turn existing speech data into synthetic "Alexa" utterances
- Applied to thousands of hours of "found" public domain audio
- Achieved 0.9% FRR (false reject rate) parity with human-collected data
- This approach eliminated the need for large-scale crowdsourced recording campaigns

**Key lesson:** Amazon solved the wake word data collection problem by converting *existing* audio rather than collecting new recordings.

## 2. The Synthetic Pipeline in Detail (openWakeWord / Piper)

This is the most documented open-source pipeline and directly usable for your project.

### Step-by-Step (13-step pipeline from openwakeword-trainer)

| Step | What Happens |
|---|---|
| 1–2 | Verify environment, apply compatibility patches |
| 3–5 | Download datasets (negatives from HuggingFace), fetch Piper TTS models, resolve paths |
| 6–7 | **Generate positive samples** — Piper TTS synthesizes N clips of the wake phrase across 50+ voices |
| 8 | Verify generated clips for quality (silence check, duration check) |
| 9 | **Augment** — add real-world noise, music, room impulse responses; extract mel-spectrogram features |
| 10 | **Train DNN classifier** — small feedforward net on top of pretrained audio embeddings |
| 11 | Export to ONNX (~200 KB) |
| 12–13 | Load-test and deploy |

**Duration:** ~1–2 hours with GPU, 12–24 hours CPU-only.

### What Piper Generates

Given the text "Hey Temple":
- 50+ different synthetic voice profiles (male, female, different ages, accents)
- Each voice generates 100–1,000 samples of the phrase
- Speed variation: 0.8× to 1.2× natural pace
- Pitch variation across samples
- Speaker embedding interpolation creates "hybrid" voices not in the original TTS training set

**Default config:** 50,000 positive training clips for one wake word.

### Why Synthetic Works (Despite Sounding Unnatural)

The audio embeddings are learned from real audio (pretrained by Google). The DNN classifier only needs to learn "what pattern in embedding space = wake word." Synthetic speech hits the same embedding clusters as real speech for the same phonemes — especially for stop consonants and vowels, which are your surviving BCM frequencies anyway.

## 3. Isolated Word vs. In-Sentence: What the Research Says

This is a critical question for your corpus design.

**Bottom line: train on both, but weight isolated heavily for the trigger model.**

| Training Data Type | Use For | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Isolated wake word ("Hey Temple.") | Wake word detector itself | Primary training signal — the model must learn the exact phoneme sequence |
| Wake word + command ("Hey Temple, start activity.") | Detecting activation at start of compound utterance | The transition from wake word into following speech changes the acoustic end boundary |
| Embedded mid-sentence ("I said hey temple to test it") | Negative examples only | You do NOT want the wake word firing when the phrase appears mid-conversation |
| Whispered / soft voice | Robustness test set | Bone conduction picks up whispers differently — important for your BCM use case |

**SPIE research finding:** Word recognition accuracy of **99.3%** on purely isolated speech vs **95.1%** when the same word was embedded in unconstrained extraneous speech. This 4% gap is the cost of not including in-context training data.

**Practical recommendation:** Train on isolated + with-command utterances. Use mid-sentence appearances as hard negatives (explicitly labeled as non-trigger even though the phoneme sequence is identical).

## 4. What Makes a Good Wake Word (Phonetic Design)

Since you're designing "Hey Temple" and "OK Temple" — here's the research-backed criteria.

### Optimal Properties

| Property | Recommendation | Hey Temple | OK Temple |
|---|---|---|---|
| Syllable count | 3–5 syllables ideal | 3 ✅ | 4 ✅ |
| Starts with plosive or sonorant | /h/, /k/, /g/, /b/, /m/ good onsets | /h/ ✅ | /oʊ/ ✅ |
| Contains diphthong or distinct vowel | "ey", "oi", "ow" — acoustically distinctive | "Hey" /eɪ/ ✅ | "OK" /oʊ.keɪ/ ✅ |
| Ends distinctively | Final consonant or syllable that doesn't blend into the next word easily | /l/ in "Temple" ✅ | /l/ in "Temple" ✅ |
| Trochaic stress (STRONG-weak) | "ÁLexa", "HEY-temple" | "HEY tem-ple" ✅ | "o-KAY tem-ple" ⚠️ slight iambic |
| Phonetically rare sequence | The full sequence should not appear often in normal speech | Moderate — "Temple" is a real word | Same |
| Distinct from command vocabulary | Wake word phonemes should not heavily overlap with your intent vocabulary | ✅ | ✅ |

### The BCM Factor for Wake Words

Your 1,500 Hz ceiling actually helps with wake word detection in one way: "Hey" and "OK" both rely heavily on vowel energy (F1 range), which your BCM captures cleanly. "Temple" has strong /m/ (nasal murmur, 250–500 Hz) and /p/ (stop burst). This is a solid BCM-compatible wake word.

The risk: final /l/ in "Temple" has an extra formant at ~1,500 Hz — right at your edge. In practice this likely doesn't matter because the rest of the word is so distinct.

## 5. False Accept vs. False Reject: The Core Tradeoff

Every wake word engine lives on this tradeoff.

- **False reject (miss):** User says the wake word — device doesn't wake up. Frustrating, breaks trust.
- **False accept:** Something else activates the device. Annoying, can be a privacy concern.

**Industry targets:**
- False reject rate (FRR): < 5% (miss fewer than 1 in 20 wake word attempts)
- False accept rate (FAR): < 0.5 per hour of ambient audio

**What drives false accepts:**
- Confusable phrases — words/phrases that sound like your wake word to the model
- For "Hey Temple": "Hey, let me think", "The temporal lobe", "Hey, tell me"

**The confusable phrase list is your most valuable negative training asset.** Build it deliberately. Run your trained model against normal conversational speech and catalog every false trigger. Those become your next batch of negatives.

## 6. What This Means for Your `stt_corpus.json`

### Current JSON design assessment

Your `wake_words` section has:
- `isolated` — ✅ correct, needed
- `with_command` — ✅ correct, needed
- `negatives` — ✅ correct, critical

**What to add/change:**

1. **The `isolated` utterances with repeated identical text are for generating multiple real human recordings — this is fine for crowdsourced data. But for model training, the primary source should be synthetic (Piper TTS). The human recordings serve as evaluation and fine-tuning data.**

2. **Add a `confusables` sub-array** — explicitly named confusable phrases (not just phonetically similar distractors). These are phrases that historically triggered false accepts during testing.

3. **`min_recordings_per_speaker` for wake words should be higher** — but the human recordings are really for evaluation. For synthetic training, you generate 50,000+ clips programmatically.

4. **Add a `synthetic_generation` metadata block** — notes on TTS voices used, augmentation settings, so the pipeline is documented at the data level.

### Recommended update to wake_word entries

```json
{
  "id": "WKW-001",
  "phrase": "Hey Temple",
  "syllables": 3,
  "trigger": "device_activation",
  "phonetic_transcription": "heɪ ˈtɛm.pəl",
  "bcm_compatible": true,
  "bcm_note": "Both syllables have strong sub-1500Hz energy — /h/ onset, /eɪ/ diphthong, /m/ nasal, /p/ stop, /ə/ schwa, /l/ liquid",
  "min_human_recordings_per_speaker": 10,
  "human_recordings_purpose": "evaluation and fine-tuning only — bulk training uses synthetic",
  "synthetic_target_samples": 50000,
  "isolated": { ... },
  "with_command": { ... },
  "negatives": { ... },
  "confusables": [
    "Hey, let me think about this.",
    "The temporal lobe controls memory.",
    "Hey, tell me about the temple ruins.",
    "The temperature is dropping fast.",
    "A simple solution would be better."
  ]
}
```

## 8. Recommended Pipeline for "Hey Temple"

Given your BCM device and existing data collection app:

1. **Generate synthetic positives** using openWakeWord + Piper TTS
   - Target: 50,000 synthetic clips of "Hey Temple" and "OK Temple" each
   - Use all available Piper voices + speaker embedding interpolation
   - Augment with room impulse responses and noise (include bone-conduction-style low-pass filter in augmentation chain to simulate your BCM)

2. **Download negative data** from openWakeWord's pre-built datasets (30,000 hours, HuggingFace)

3. **Collect human recordings from your app users** (use as evaluation set, not training)
   - 10 isolated recordings per user
   - 5 with-command recordings per user
   - Store separately, tagged `purpose: evaluation`

4. **Build confusable phrase list** — collect recordings of phonetically similar sentences, add to negatives

5. **Train** using openWakeWord training notebook (~2 hours on GPU)

6. **Evaluate** against human recordings — target < 5% FRR, < 0.5 FAR/hour

7. **BCM-specific step:** Re-augment your synthetic positives through a 1,500 Hz low-pass filter before training — this simulates what your microphone will actually deliver. A model trained on full-bandwidth synthetic audio will degrade on BCM input.
