Five Ways to Ignite Curiosity

The day a child stops asking why is never their fault.
These five sessions are an attempt to make sure that day never comes.

01
The Hidden Design in Every Object

Engineering

"Take out your pen. That's an engineering object. The ball at the tip is tungsten carbide — one of the hardest materials on earth. Someone decided that. Why?"

Design Thinking Cycle Inside a Fan & LED Why Triangles Hold Your Smartphone's Guts Why Things Fail Materials Science
What We Explore

The fan (rotating magnetic field), the LED (why it replaced the bulb at 5% vs 90% efficiency), the bicycle (gear ratios + the triangle), the smartphone (15 billion transistors on your fingernail). The design cycle: every useful thing failed hundreds of times before working.

Possible Activities
Disassemble a ballpoint pen — name every part, rebuild it Paper bridge — one A4 sheet, no tape, load with coins Egg drop — protect a raw egg from 2m with rubber bands + straws Straw tower — tallest freestanding structure wins Sketch the inside of a fan from imagination, compare to reality
✦

They'll leave wonderingEvery object in their house will start looking different — as something that was designed, tested, and failed before it worked. They'll notice what they never noticed before.

02
Eight Stories. Equal Representation. No Mythology.

Scientists & Their Stories

"Textbooks say Newton saw an apple fall. That Einstein was always brilliant. Almost none of it is true. Here are the real stories."

Newton Marie Curie Ramanujan Rosalind Franklin APJ Kalam Tessy Thomas Nikola Tesla Minal Dakhave
What We Explore

Each story told honestly: Ramanujan was unemployed and self-taught. Franklin took the DNA image and got no credit. Kalam sold newspapers as a child. Tesla died broke while every wall socket uses his invention. 4 women, 4 men, 3 Indians.

Possible Activities
Match 8 quotes to scientists — no names given Students write one problem in their village they wish someone would solve Timeline on the floor — rope from 1600 to today, place each scientist Debate: who deserved the Nobel Prize — Watson or Franklin? Pick a scientist, write 3 sentences: problem, discovery, why it matters
✦

They'll leave wonderingScientists are not a different species. Every single one came from somewhere ordinary, faced something unfair, and kept going. So can they.

03
From Sputnik to Chandrayaan-3

Space & Technology

"In 1957, a metal ball the size of a basketball was launched into space. It just beeped. And it terrified the most powerful nation on earth."

The Space Race How Rockets Work ISRO's Journey Satellites & GPS Chandrayaan-3 Mars & Beyond
What We Explore

Newton's 3rd law as the physics of rockets. ISRO from a bicycle-transported rocket in 1963 to the Moon's south pole in 2023. How GPS, weather forecasts, and cyclone warnings all run on satellites. Mangalyaan cost less than the film Gravity.

Possible Activities
Balloon rocket on a string across the room — Newton's 3rd law live Compare Mangalyaan's ₹450 cr budget to Bollywood blockbuster budgets Design a 6-month Mars mission — what 10 things do you pack? GPS triangulation on board — draw 3 circles, find the intersection Calculate how far light travels in 1 second, 1 minute, 8 minutes (Sun)
✦

They'll leave wonderingIndia landed on the Moon's south pole before Russia, China, or anyone else — on a budget smaller than a Hollywood film. What else is ISRO building right now?

04
Ancient Navigation to Modern Cosmology

Stars & the Solar System

"Before clocks and GPS, how did farmers know when to plant? How did sailors cross oceans? They used the same tool: the sky above them."

Why Humans Studied the Sky Scale of the Universe What Stars Actually Are The Solar System Big Bang to Today Are We Alone?
What We Explore

Why ancient civilisations built observatories. Scale of space (Sun as a coin — nearest star is 420 km away at that scale). What stars are made of and how they die. Dark matter. The Big Bang. Whether life exists elsewhere.

Possible Activities
Scale model walk in school field — planets as seeds and marbles Pinhole projector — observe the Sun safely on paper Draw your own constellation — connect dot field into anything you see Night observation — identify Saptarshi, visible planets, Milky Way "You are stardust" — trace which elements in your body came from stars
✦

They'll leave wonderingEvery atom of iron in their blood was forged inside a star that exploded before our Sun was born. "We are stardust" is not poetry — it's chemistry.

05
The Kitchen as a Chemistry Lab

Science of Food

"You've eaten thousands of meals. But do you know why bread puffs up, why onions make you cry, or why yesterday's sabzi tastes better than today's?"

Maillard Reaction Fermentation Acid–Base Chemistry Heat Transfer Emulsification Food Preservation
What We Explore

Why cooking changes food at a molecular level. Why dahi forms and spoiled milk doesn't. Why baking soda makes cake fluffy. Why lemon preserves food. Why the pressure cooker cooks faster. Why honey never spoils.

Possible Activities
Yeast balloon — sugar + warm water + yeast inflates a balloon with CO₂ Make butter by shaking cream in a sealed bottle for 10 minutes Test kitchen liquids as acid/base using turmeric as indicator Caramelise sugar on a spoon over flame — watch white → golden → black Cook the same vegetable two ways, taste and compare
✦

They'll leave wonderingEvery time they eat something, they'll have a question about why it tastes the way it does. The kitchen will never look the same again.
