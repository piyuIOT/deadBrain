## Activity 1: Measurement Units
**Time:** 60 min
**Materials:** Pencil, eraser, small rope, etc.

- Recap: units are conventions — wall length can be measured in pencil lengths
- e.g. wall = 1m long, pencil = 10cm → wall = 10 pencils
- Standard units (meter) allow communication between people
- Place a box with objects (pencil, eraser, short rope) in class centre — students measure walls, blackboard, water bottle using these as units
- Different objects chosen so students must use different units (e.g. rope for width of bottle)
- Only 1 of each object in box → students must exchange
- Groups of 2; 30 min for measuring
- At end: measure everything with meter tape; students convert their non-standard measurements to standard

**Table:**
| # | Object | Unit used | Measurement | Standard (meter) |
|---|--------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Wall | Pencil | 10 pencils | 1 m |

**Discussion:** Why do different teams get different answers? Smaller unit = more accurate. What difficulties arose?

## Activity 2: Heart Clock (दिल की घड़ी)
**Time:** 60 min
**Materials:** Stopwatches, laptop

- Recap: time is measured by periodic motion (simple oscillator)
- Examples of periodic phenomena: moon cycles (month), Earth's orbit (year), heartbeat
- Students measure their heartbeat period: count 10 beats with stopwatch, divide by 10
- Teacher plays a popular song; students estimate song length using heartbeat count
- Convert heartbeat count → seconds using their heartbeat period
- Discussion: why do different students get different answers? (heartbeat varies person to person and over time — not as precise as a clock, but approximate)
- Repeat for 2–3 songs

**Key concept:** Periodic motion → time measurement. Heartbeat is a periodic event.

Student notes captured in class: Motion = when position of object changes with time. Examples: running, jumping, swimming, eating, drinking. Periodic motion = when object returns to its original position at fixed intervals.

## Activity 3: Accurate Race (सटीक दौड़)
**Time:** 70 min
**Materials:** Stopwatches, meter scale

- Teach average speed calculation
- The winner is NOT the fastest runner — the winner is whoever estimates their own time most accurately
- Announce race distance (e.g. 100m)
- Each student uses stopwatch + meter scale to measure their average speed (run a short distance, time it, repeat multiple times)
- Use average speed to predict time for full race distance → note everyone's prediction
- Run the race one by one, record actual time
- Compare prediction vs actual → winner = closest estimate
- Discuss: how to improve accuracy, where did error come from?

## Activity 4: Distance-Time Graph
**Time:** 80 min
**Materials:** Stopwatches, meter scale, blackboard

- Students seated 1m apart along the track, each with a stopwatch
- Teacher (or another person) runs 5 times past them
- Each student records time when runner passes their position
- 4 teams create obstacles before runs 2–5; run 1 = no obstacle (baseline)
- Obstacle = friction analog (objects placed in path or students sitting)
- After all runs: plot distance-time graphs on blackboard (1 point per student per run)

**Sample data (run times):**
| Run # | Time (sec) |
|-------|-----------|
| 1 (no obstacle) | 20 |
| 2 | 35 |
| 3 | 40 |
| 4 | 30 |
| 5 | 25 |

**Discussion:**
- Which team won (slowed runner most)?
- Run 1 graph = straight line (constant speed, no friction) vs other runs
- Steeper slope = slower speed
- Obstacle acts like friction force
- Best obstacle = one that minimized slope most

**Key concepts from student notes:**
- Force types: contact force (संपर्क बल), non-contact force (असम्पर्क बल)
- Muscular force (पेशीय बल), magnetic force (चुम्बकीय बल)
- Friction (घर्षण बल) = force between two surfaces in contact
- Pressure (दाब) = force per unit area

## Activity 5: Tug of War (रस्साकशी)
**Time:** 40 min
**Materials:** Rope

- Recap friction chapter; introduce tug of war
- Discussion: where does friction appear? (hands gripping rope, feet on ground)
- How to increase/decrease friction to win? Reducing opponent's foot-ground friction is advantageous (use oil, sand)
- Divide into 2 teams; 10 min before each match to modify friction (oil on opponent's rope, sand on their side, etc.)
- 3 matches total
- Post-game discussion: force, pressure (दाब = force applied on unit area of a surface). Is friction a force?
